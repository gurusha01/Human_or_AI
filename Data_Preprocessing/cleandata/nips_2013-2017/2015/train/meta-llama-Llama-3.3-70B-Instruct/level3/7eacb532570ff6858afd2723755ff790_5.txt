This paper presents a novel approach to object detection, introducing Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that share convolutional features with the detection network, enabling nearly cost-free region proposals. The authors propose a fully-convolutional network that predicts object bounds and objectness scores at each position, which can be trained end-to-end for the task of generating high-quality region proposals.
The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' ideas. The introduction provides a good background on the current state of object detection and the motivation for the proposed approach. The related work section is comprehensive, discussing various existing methods for region proposal and object detection.
The technical contributions of the paper are significant, as the authors propose a new architecture for RPNs and a simple training scheme that alternates between fine-tuning for the region proposal task and fine-tuning for object detection. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, achieving state-of-the-art object detection accuracy on PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012 benchmarks.
However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors do not provide a thorough discussion on the regret bounds of the proposed algorithm, which is an important aspect of dueling bandit algorithms. Additionally, the experiments are conducted on synthetic data, which may not accurately reflect real-world scenarios. The use of synthetic data makes it unclear when the Plackett-Luce distribution assumption holds and the applicability of the proposed approach.
In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques, as the authors combine the ideas of region proposal networks and fully-convolutional networks to create a new architecture for object detection. The paper also provides a unique perspective on the problem of object detection, highlighting the importance of sharing convolutional features between the region proposal and detection networks.
The significance of the paper lies in its potential to advance the state of the art in object detection, as the proposed approach achieves high accuracy and speed. The paper also provides a new perspective on the problem of object detection, which can inspire future research in this area.
Overall, the paper is well-written, and the technical contributions are significant. However, the lack of discussion on regret bounds and the use of synthetic data are notable limitations. With some revisions to address these limitations, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of object detection.
Arguments for acceptance:
* The paper presents a novel approach to object detection, which achieves state-of-the-art accuracy and speed.
* The technical contributions are significant, as the authors propose a new architecture for RPNs and a simple training scheme.
* The paper provides a unique perspective on the problem of object detection, highlighting the importance of sharing convolutional features between the region proposal and detection networks.

* The use of synthetic data makes it unclear when the Plackett-Luce distribution assumption holds and the applicability of the proposed approach.
