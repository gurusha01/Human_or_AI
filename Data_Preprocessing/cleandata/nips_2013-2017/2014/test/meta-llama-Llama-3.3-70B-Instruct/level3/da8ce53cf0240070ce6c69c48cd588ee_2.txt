This paper proposes a novel framework for causal strategic inference in networked microfinance economies, which enables the evaluation of various intervention policies. The authors model a microfinance market as a two-sided market consisting of microfinance institutions (MFIs) and villages, and develop an algorithm to compute the equilibrium interest rates and loan allocations. The paper also presents an empirical study based on data from Bolivia and Bangladesh, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed framework in predicting the outcomes of different interventions.
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a good balance between theoretical and empirical contributions. The authors provide a thorough review of the literature on microfinance and causal inference, and their model is well-motivated by the characteristics of microfinance markets. The empirical study is also well-designed, with a careful selection of data sources and a thorough analysis of the results.
One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide insights into the effects of different intervention policies, such as the removal of government-owned MFIs or the imposition of an interest rate ceiling. The authors' findings are consistent with empirical evidence and provide valuable guidance for policymakers. The paper also makes a significant contribution to the literature on causal inference in strategic settings, which is a relatively new and rapidly growing field.
However, there are some limitations to the paper. The model assumes that the villages have a simple objective function, which may not capture the complexity of real-world decision-making. Additionally, the paper relies on a number of assumptions, such as the absence of default risk and the existence of a unique equilibrium point, which may not always hold in practice. The empirical study is also limited to two countries, and it would be useful to see the results replicated in other contexts.
