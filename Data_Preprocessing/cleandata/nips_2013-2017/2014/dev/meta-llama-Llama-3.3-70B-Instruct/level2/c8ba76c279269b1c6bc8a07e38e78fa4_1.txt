This paper presents a novel computational model of the locust olfactory system, which is capable of recovering high-dimensional sparse olfactory signals from a small number of measurements. The authors propose that the early olfactory system acts as a spectrum analyzer, using maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference to recover the sparse odor vector responsible for the glomerular observations. They show that solving a dual formulation of the corresponding optimization problem yields structure and dynamics in good agreement with biological data.
The paper's main claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and simulations. The authors demonstrate that the full dual circuit, which uses independent component analysis (ICA) to continuously adapt to the olfactory environment, can perfectly recover binary odor vectors. They also show that the reduced dual circuit, which replaces the reciprocal interaction of the PNs with the KCs via the matrix A with interaction with the M LNs via the square matrix B, can achieve better performance than a feedforward circuit.

The paper also includes a number of predictions and suggestions for future work, which demonstrates the authors' commitment to advancing the field.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main limitations is the assumption that the odor environment is static and unchanging, which may not be realistic in many cases. The authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest that future work could focus on developing more dynamic models of the olfactory system.
