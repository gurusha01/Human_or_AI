The paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, such as Goofspiel and Tron. It builds on prior work (Lanctot et al., 2013) by advancing the theoretical understanding of MCTS in this domain, particularly by proving convergence to approximate Nash equilibria when using -Hannan consistent selection methods like regret matching and Exp3. The authors also provide empirical evidence supporting their theoretical claims, demonstrating convergence on randomly generated and worst-case games. This work addresses a gap in the literature by extending MCTS analysis beyond sequential games to simultaneous-move settings.
Strengths:
1. Technical Quality: The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs demonstrating convergence to approximate Nash equilibria. The inductive proof approach, starting with optimal strategies at leaf nodes and extending to inner nodes, is well-structured and insightful.
2. Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making complex concepts accessible. Definitions, algorithms, and proofs are presented systematically, and the empirical results are effectively visualized.
3. Originality: The work is novel in its formal analysis of MCTS in simultaneous-move games, a previously underexplored area. It generalizes existing results and provides a theoretical foundation for using MCTS in this broader class of games.
4. Significance: The results are important for both theoretical and practical applications. The convergence guarantees justify the use of MCTS in simultaneous-move games, and the empirical evaluation highlights its robustness. This work has the potential to influence future research in game theory and AI.
Weaknesses:
1. Limited Analysis of Inner Nodes: While the paper provides strong results for leaf nodes, the analysis of inner nodes is less comprehensive. The inductive approach assumes bounded errors but does not deeply explore the dynamics of subgame play at these nodes.
2. Connection to Subgame Perfect Nash Equilibria: The paper does not explicitly tie its results to the concept of subgame perfect Nash equilibria, which would strengthen its theoretical contributions. Citing and building on foundational work like Harris (1990) could provide additional depth.
Suggestions:
To address the weaknesses, the authors could:
1. Expand the analysis of inner nodes to provide more detailed insights into their role in convergence.
2. Explicitly connect the results to subgame perfect Nash equilibria, incorporating relevant citations (e.g., Harris, 1990) to contextualize the findings within broader game-theoretic frameworks.
Recommendation:
The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by extending MCTS analysis to simultaneous-move games and proving convergence to approximate Nash equilibria. Its theoretical rigor and empirical validation make it a strong candidate for acceptance. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further enhance its impact. Accept with minor revisions.
