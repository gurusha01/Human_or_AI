This paper proposes a novel supervised topic model, called the Relevance Topic Model (RTM), for recognizing unstructured social group activities in web videos. The RTM integrates sparse Bayesian learning with an undirected topic model to discover sparse relevance topics that are associated with class labels. The model uses rectified linear units to better fit complex video data and facilitates the learning of the model. The authors also develop an efficient variational EM algorithm for parameter estimation and inference.
The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise overview of the proposed model and its components. The related work section is thorough, and the authors provide a good discussion of the differences between their approach and existing supervised topic models. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the RTM in recognizing social group activities, particularly in the scenario of lacking labeled training videos.
The strengths of the paper include:
* The proposal of a novel supervised topic model that integrates sparse Bayesian learning with an undirected topic model.
* The use of rectified linear units to better fit complex video data.
* The development of an efficient variational EM algorithm for parameter estimation and inference.
* The demonstration of the effectiveness of the RTM in recognizing social group activities, particularly in the scenario of lacking labeled training videos.
The weaknesses of the paper include:
* The paper assumes that the number of topics is fixed and known in advance, which may not be the case in practice.
* The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential avenues for future research.
Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed model is novel and effective. The authors provide a clear and concise overview of the model and its components, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the RTM in recognizing social group activities.
