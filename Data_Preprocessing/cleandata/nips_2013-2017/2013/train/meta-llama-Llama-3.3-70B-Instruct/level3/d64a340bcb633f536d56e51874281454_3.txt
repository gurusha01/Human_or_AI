This paper proposes a novel approach to nearest-neighbor search using binary space-partitioning trees (BSP-trees). The authors establish a theoretical connection between the search performance of BSP-trees and their vector quantization performance, demonstrating that trees with better quantization performance have better search performance guarantees. They also introduce the concept of large margin partitions, which can improve search performance by reducing the error bounds.
The paper's strengths include its rigorous theoretical analysis, which provides a deeper understanding of the relationship between BSP-tree search performance and vector quantization. The authors also provide empirical evidence to support their claims, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach on several datasets. The introduction of the max-margin tree (MM-tree) is a significant contribution, as it explicitly maximizes the margin of every partition, leading to improved search performance.
However, there are some weaknesses to the paper. The setup for the primary result, Theorem 4.1, is complex and could be augmented with a more comprehensive description and example dictionaries. Additionally, the paper could benefit from further empirical evidence, explanations, and comparisons to existing methods like Lasso to demonstrate its practical value. The authors also assume that the data lies in a growth-restricted metric space, which may not always be the case in practice.
In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful to evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides enough information for an expert reader to reproduce the results.
The originality of the paper lies in its novel approach to nearest-neighbor search using BSP-trees and the introduction of the MM-tree. The authors provide a unique combination of theoretical and empirical results, which sets their work apart from previous contributions.
The significance of the paper is evident in its potential to improve the state of the art in nearest-neighbor search. The authors' results have implications for various applications, including computer vision, machine learning, and data mining. The paper's contributions are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners in these fields.
Arguments for acceptance:
* The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the relationship between BSP-tree search performance and vector quantization.
* The introduction of the MM-tree is a significant contribution to the field.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the authors' approach on several datasets.
Arguments against acceptance:
* The setup for Theorem 4.1 is complex and could be improved.
* The paper could benefit from further empirical evidence and comparisons to existing methods.
* The authors' assumptions about the data may not always be valid in practice.
Overall, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides a significant contribution to the field of nearest-neighbor search. With some revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, the paper has the potential to be a strong addition to the conference program.
