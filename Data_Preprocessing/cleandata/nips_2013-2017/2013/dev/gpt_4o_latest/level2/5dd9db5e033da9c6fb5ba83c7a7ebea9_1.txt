This paper presents a novel approach to submodular function minimization (SFM) by framing it as a best approximation problem and leveraging reflection-based optimization methods. The authors propose three main algorithms—dual block-coordinate descent, Douglas-Rachford splitting, and accelerated projected gradient methods—and demonstrate their efficiency in terms of convergence speed, ease of implementation, and parallelizability. The paper's primary contributions include the development of a proximal problem formulation, the use of reflection methods to avoid cumbersome hyperparameter tuning, and empirical validation on image segmentation tasks.
Strengths
1. Technical Soundness: The paper is well-grounded in theory, with clear derivations of the dual formulations and their connections to the proximal problem. The use of convex duality and decomposition techniques is rigorous and builds on established concepts like the Lovász extension and base polytopes.
2. Novelty: The approach is innovative in its use of reflection methods (e.g., Douglas-Rachford splitting) for SFM, which avoids hyperparameter tuning—a common bottleneck in existing methods. This is a significant improvement over traditional subgradient and smoothing-based methods.
3. Practical Utility: The proposed methods are shown to be highly parallelizable, making them suitable for large-scale problems. The experiments demonstrate competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art graph-cut algorithms, with promising speedups in parallel settings.
4. Clarity and Reproducibility: The paper is well-organized, with detailed explanations of the algorithms and their theoretical underpinnings. The inclusion of code and data for reproducibility is commendable.
5. Empirical Validation: The experiments are thorough, covering both graph-cut problems and combinations of concave and cut functions. The results convincingly show the advantages of the proposed methods, particularly the Douglas-Rachford splitting method, in terms of convergence speed and parallel efficiency.
Weaknesses
1. Limited Scope of Applications: While the paper focuses on image segmentation tasks, it would benefit from broader applications to demonstrate the generality of the approach. For example, testing on other domains like sensor placement or document summarization could strengthen the paper's impact.
2. Comparison with Specialized Algorithms: Although the authors compare their methods to generic SFM approaches, a more detailed comparison with specialized graph-cut algorithms (e.g., [7]) in terms of scalability and accuracy would provide deeper insights.
3. Parallel Implementation Details: While the paper highlights the parallelizability of the methods, it does not provide sufficient details on the parallel implementation or scalability beyond 8 cores. A more extensive analysis of parallel performance would enhance the practical relevance.
Arguments for Acceptance
- The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem, with significant improvements in ease of implementation and convergence speed.
- The empirical results are compelling, showing that the proposed methods outperform existing approaches in key metrics.
- The work is highly relevant to the NeurIPS community, given its focus on optimization techniques for machine learning and computer vision tasks.
Arguments Against Acceptance
- The scope of applications is somewhat narrow, limiting the generalizability of the results.
- The experimental comparisons with specialized algorithms are not exhaustive, leaving questions about the practical trade-offs in real-world scenarios.
Recommendation
I recommend acceptance of this paper, as it makes a strong theoretical and practical contribution to the field of submodular optimization. Addressing the noted weaknesses in future work could further enhance its impact.
