This paper presents a novel approach to deconvolve feedback loops in recommender systems, allowing for the recovery of true user preferences without the influence of the system's recommendations. The authors propose a set of assumptions that enable the creation of a tractable model of a recommender system, which is then used to develop an algorithm for deconvolving feedback loops. The algorithm is based on singular value decomposition (SVD) of the observed ratings matrix and is able to recover the true ratings matrix up to a scaling constant.
The paper is well-written and well-organized, making it easy to follow and understand. The authors provide a clear explanation of their assumptions and methodology, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner. The use of synthetic and real-world datasets to validate the approach is also a strength of the paper.
One of the key strengths of the paper is the development of a metric to assess the extent of recommender system effects on the final ratings matrix. This metric is based on the idea that deviation of ratings from a straight line suggests recommender effects, and the authors use a random sample consensus (RANSAC) method to fit a straight line to the observed and deconvolved ratings. The resulting score is able to identify items that are likely to be influenced by the recommender system.
The paper also presents some interesting results on the analysis of real-world datasets, including the identification of items that are likely to be influenced by the recommender system and the ranking of items based on their recommendation score. The authors also provide some insights into the differences between various datasets, including the presence or absence of recommender system effects.
However, there are some limitations to the paper. One potential limitation is that the assumptions made by the authors may not always hold in practice. For example, the assumption that the user mean and item norms are roughly equal may not be true in all cases. Additionally, the authors' approach relies on the availability of a single snapshot of the ratings matrix, which may not always be available.
In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to deconvolving feedback loops in recommender systems. The use of SVD and the development of a metric to assess recommender system effects are both new contributions. The paper also provides a unique perspective on the problem of recovering true user preferences in the presence of feedback loops.
The significance of the paper lies in its ability to provide insights into the extent of recommender system effects on user ratings. This has important implications for the design of recommender systems, as it suggests that the system's recommendations can have a significant impact on user behavior. The paper also provides a framework for analyzing and understanding the effects of feedback loops in recommender systems, which is an important area of research.

The results are interesting and provide insights into the extent of recommender system effects on user ratings.
