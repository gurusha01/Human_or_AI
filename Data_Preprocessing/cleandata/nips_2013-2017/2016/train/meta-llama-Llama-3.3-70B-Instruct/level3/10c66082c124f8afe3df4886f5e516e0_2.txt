This paper proposes a novel approach to domain adaptation in deep networks, which enables the joint learning of adaptive classifiers and transferable features from labeled data in the source domain and unlabeled data in the target domain. The authors relax the shared-classifier assumption made by previous methods and assume that the source classifier and target classifier differ by a residual function. They enable classifier adaptation by plugging several layers into deep networks to explicitly learn the residual function with reference to the target classifier.
The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand the proposed approach. The authors provide a thorough review of related work, highlighting the limitations of existing domain adaptation methods and motivating the need for a new approach. The proposed Residual Transfer Network (RTN) approach is well-explained, and the authors provide a clear description of the architecture and the training procedure.
The experimental evaluation is extensive and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach on several benchmark datasets. The results show that the RTN approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods, including Deep Domain Confusion (DDC), Deep Adaptation Network (DAN), and Reverse Gradient (RevGrad). The authors also provide an ablation study to evaluate the contribution of each component of the RTN approach, which helps to understand the importance of each module.
The paper has several strengths, including:
* The proposed RTN approach is novel and addresses a key limitation of existing domain adaptation methods, which assume that the source classifier and target classifier are identical.
* The approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the underlying assumptions and the architecture of the RTN model.
* The experimental evaluation is extensive and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach on several benchmark datasets.
* The authors provide a thorough review of related work, highlighting the limitations of existing domain adaptation methods and motivating the need for a new approach.
However, the paper also has some weaknesses, including:
* The proposed approach requires careful tuning of hyperparameters, which can be time-consuming and may not be feasible in all scenarios.
* The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed approach, which may be important for large-scale applications.
* The paper does not address the issue of semi-supervised domain adaptation, which is an important scenario in many real-world applications.
